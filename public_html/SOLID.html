SOLID:

In the field of programming, SOLID (Single Function, Open and Closed Principle, Richterian Alternation, Interface Isolation, and Dependency Reversal) is a mnemonic acronym introduced by Robert C. Martin in the early 21st century that refers to five basic principles of object-oriented programming and object-oriented design. When applied together, these principles make it possible for a programmer to develop a system that is easy to maintain and extend with software. SOLID is typically used in test-driven development and is an important component of the fundamental principles of agile and adaptive software development.

S Single Function Principle The concept that "an object should have only one function".

O Open and Closed Principle The concept that "software should be open to extensions, but closed to modifications".

L The Richter replacement principle holds that "an object in a program should be replaceable by its subclasses without changing the correctness of the program". See Contractual Design.

I Interface Isolation Principle The notion that "multiple client-specific interfaces are better than one broad-use interface" .

D Dependency Inversion Principle The notion that a method should follow "dependency on an abstraction rather than an instance" .
